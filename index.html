Executive Summary

GoFlight is a charter operations platform for mid-size US operators — the 10-to-30-aircraft owner-operators who run their own booking, manage their own trips, and are drowning in coordination work their existing tools can't do. Our agent is named Flyn: an always-on teammate that absorbs the busywork — chasing vendors, tracking trips, propagating changes, collecting documents, flagging what's missing — so the dispatcher and the owner get their week back.

What GoFlight is not: a marketplace, a quoting engine, or an Avinode replacement. GoFlight lives in the workflow that happens after the quote is accepted. And there is no forced AI: the customer never sees a Flyn persona. Messages go out under the operator's team name, from their brand. Flyn is invisible to clients — it just looks like the team got faster.

The Scene

It's Friday at 4:30pm. A dispatcher at a mid-size charter operator is on her third phone call in ten minutes. The 6pm out of Teterboro just added a passenger. Flight attendant staffing is now wrong. Catering needs to change. The FBO needs updated manifest paperwork before wheels-up. The customer texted an hour ago asking for a status update she hasn't had time to send. There's a quote request sitting in her inbox from a broker she's been trying to cultivate for months, and she won't get to it until Monday.

It's not that today is a bad day. Today is every day.

Multiply this dispatcher by 10–30 aircraft and the picture is the US mid-size charter operator: trips closing because of heroic effort, not because the operations platform is helping. Charter runs on time. Every trip is a collection of windows, deadlines, and handoffs — and when the coordination work takes too long, trips slip, customers get frustrated, and teams burn out. The relational half of the work (customer judgment, operator relationships, crew rapport) is genuinely hard and genuinely human. The logistical half (vendor coordination, document collection, trip tracking, change propagation across eight parties) is the part nobody went into aviation for.

Why the category is broken

The tools exist. Avinode, FL3XX, and the enterprise platforms do quoting, fleet visibility, and compliance reporting well. What they don't do is the coordination work itself. Their seat-license pricing is also out of reach for most broker-operators, which means the teams that most need help can't afford the tools that were supposed to provide it. Even the operators who can pay still find themselves back in WhatsApp for the actual day-to-day.

The dispatcher still chases the caterer manually. The owner-operator still updates the FBO by text. The trip state still lives in her head, a spreadsheet, and a WhatsApp thread with the crew. The category spent a decade building control panels for a job that needed a colleague.

A dashboard can show you the work. It cannot do it.

Until recently, that was true of any software.

Mission & Strategy

What GoFlight is

GoFlight is a charter operations platform for mid-size US operators — the 10-to-30-aircraft owner-operators who run their own booking, manage their own trips, and are drowning in coordination work their existing tools can't do for them. They're the operators looking for ways to grow revenue, elevate customer experience, and reduce operational chaos — and time is the thing they're running out of first.

Our agent is named Flyn. Flyn is an always-on teammate, not a feature. Flyn absorbs the coordination work that was costing both the dispatcher and the owner their week — chasing vendors, tracking trips, propagating changes, collecting documents before the trip starts, and flagging what's missing before the customer notices. Flyn doesn't replace the dispatcher's relationships or the owner's judgment. Flyn takes the time back.

No forced AI. The customer never sees a Flyn persona. Messages go out under your team's name, from your brand. Flyn is invisible to your clients — it just looks like your team got faster.

The effect is a force multiplier, not a replacement. Well-run charter teams using agent-based coordination can run meaningfully more trip volume with the same headcount. That translates to the outcomes the owner actually cares about: more revenue per seat, better customer experience, and a team that isn't dead by Friday. The dispatcher spends her day on the customer and the deal, not the vendor chase. The owner closes more trips without hiring.

Why Now

  • LLMs used to answer questions. Agents now do work. The shift is concrete: agents can call tools, handle multi-step workflows, coordinate across systems, and make decisions inside the flow — reliably, in production, in 2026. That's a different technology than the chatbots of 18 months ago. The category of agent-native vertical software is forming now, and charter is one of the cleanest places for it to land.
  • The incumbent's strength is in the wrong place. Avinode's moat is operator inventory and a 20-year network of relationships. That moat is in sourcing and quoting. The operations layer — everything that happens after the quote — is structurally unoccupied by the dominant player and under-served by the enterprise tools.
  • Charter is a coordination business more than a quoting business. Roughly half of what a dispatcher or owner-operator does each day is coordination and communication work. That is exactly where agents are strongest and exactly where existing software is weakest.
  • Salesforce did this once before. They told buyers to stop installing software and subscribe instead. The next turn is stop running software, hire an agent to do the work. Charter is where it lands first.

Who this is for

Beachhead (first 90 days). US owner-operators running 10–30 aircraft who do their own charter booking in-house. Both sides of the transaction — inventory and demand — in the same building. Fastest feedback loops, single decision-maker, highest concentration of the pain GoFlight solves. Nationally there are roughly 3,000–4,000 Part 135 certificate holders operating ~11,000+ aircraft; the 10–30 aircraft mid-market is large enough to build a real business inside, narrow enough to serve deeply with a focused team.

Expansion (months 4–12). Independent charter brokers who work with those operators. Same logistical pain, come in through existing operator relationships rather than cold acquisition.

Not a target (yet). Enterprise operators with 50+ aircraft and dedicated IT teams. Those are the customers well-funded competitors are chasing. Their procurement cycles, integration demands, and security reviews are not a fit for our stage.

Phase plan

  • Phase 1 (now — months 0–9): Operations platform for owner-operators. Win the daily workflow. Earn system-of-record status.
  • Phase 2 (months 9–18): Expansion into brokers. Same platform, brokered workflow. Come in through operator relationships.
  • Phase 3 (18+ months): Transparent marketplace built on operations data. Trip data unlocks a customer-facing front door and a marketplace that competes on truth, not price — earned by the operations layer, not built in parallel to it.

Regulatory & Compliance

Private aviation operates under FAA Part 135 certification, TSA security requirements, and DOT oversight. GoFlight's founding team has direct experience operating as a charter broker, but the full regulatory landscape for a technology platform facilitating Part 135 bookings is an area that requires deeper expertise as the company scales.

Understanding the compliance obligations that apply to GoFlight as a platform — distinct from those that apply to the operators themselves — is an active area of research and a priority as we move toward broader market adoption. We view this as an opportunity for the right partner or advisor to add significant value.
